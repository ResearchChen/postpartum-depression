  • Diagnosis Pre-treatment

  • Postpartum depression symptoms can happen within the first few weeks to months postpartum (e.g., afterbirth, miscarriage, or stillbirth).

  • Postpartum depression can disturb you emotionally and physically such as you cannot take care for or bond with your baby.

  • Postpartum depression can last for months and is more serious than postpartum "baby blues".

  • Untreated postpartum depression can lead to chronic depressive disorder. Reach out to your health providers as soon as you feel unwell. The sooner you have your symptoms under control, the sooner you will feel better and connect with your baby.

Diagnosis Pre-treatment

Early diagnosis and intervention can help to control further developments of symptoms and therefore postpartum medical checks which occur around 3 to 6 weeks postpartum are very crucial.

Your healthcare provider during the postpartum checkup will screen your moods and emotions such as questioning your postpartum feelings and assessing your risks of developing postpartum depression disorder.

Your healthcare provider will also check your thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) levels making sure your thyroid disturbance is not the reason leading to any depression symptoms.

Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

  • The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is a screening tool most commonly used in perinatal care for identifying patients with postpartum depression.
  • The scale can be used between 28 to 32 weeks in all pregnancies and 6 to 8 weeks postpartum and is a 10-item questionnaire that collects responses and feedback from patients regarding their emotions and moods.
  • Patients who scored a high score in EPDS (i.e., above 11) indicated a high possibility of postpartum depression.
  • Healthcare providers will recommend appropriate treatments if they believe their patients have signs of postpartum depression.

Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH) Test

  • Postpartum thyroid dysfunction is common among new mothers.
  • Normal TSH value ranges from 0.4 to 4.0 mIU/L.
  • People who have a TSH value above 4.0 mIU/L during labor are significantly associated with an increased risk of postpartum depression disorder six months postpartum.
  • Some people have their thyroid become inflamed after having a baby. It may first cause the thyroid to be overactive. But in time it can lead to an underactive thyroid.
  • People who have an overactive or underactive thyroid would receive other treatments specifically targeting their malfunctioning thyroid.
