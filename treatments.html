  • If you are experiencing moderate to severe postpartum depression, please reach out for support as soon as possible. Untreated postpartum depression can affect your mothering skills as well as your baby. Babies born to depressed mothers may be more irritable or less attentive than others.

  • Experiencing postpartum depression is not a reflection of your mothering skills. It is a biological condition resulting from multiple environmental and genetic factors.

  • Postpartum depression is not exclusive to mothers. 1 in 10 men encounters postpartum depression, a prevalence that is noteworthy when compared to 1 in 7 women who also experience this challenge.

Available Treatments

Psychotherapy

  • Psychotherapy (i.e., talk therapy) is helpful by having a licensed mental health professional to listen and talk through your concerns.
  • You may feel better after therapy sessions by finding more positive and effective ways to cope with your troubling feelings, solve problems, set realistic goals, and respond to situations.
  • Family or relationship therapy may also be helpful depending on your specific concerns and situations.
  • Common psychotherapies used for postpartum depression include cognitive-behavioral theory and interpersonal psychotherapy.
  • Occur in one-on-one or in a group setting.
  • People with mild postpartum depression can become better with psychotherapy alone.

Antidepressant Medicines

  • Antidepressants have a well-established history of effectively treating postpartum depression.
  • Antidepressants can help to balance and restore those disturbed and imbalanced chemicals in your brain.
  • Need to be taken for at least three to four weeks to work and are typically prescribed for a minimum of 6 months after symptoms improve.
  • Postponing and terminating medication too soon can cause a return of symptoms.
  • Antidepressants can be transferred to your baby through your milk. But the transfer level is low and many antidepressants are considered safe.
  • People with moderate to severe postpartum depression are recommended to combine psychotherapy with medicine.

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is a type of counseling focusing on changing certain thoughts and behavior patterns to control the symptoms of a condition. Participating and receiving a CBT workshop for one day targeting postpartum depression can effectively improve depressive symptoms. CBT provides a medium-sized decrease in depressive symptoms both immediately after the treatment and up to six months after treatment considered to be more effective than usual care alone (medication and other therapies).
  • Interpersonal therapy is a short-term treatment focusing on social and personal relationships and related problems. The mental health practitioner aims to help a person assess and develop effective solutions to their interpersonal skills and relationship problems. This therapy can provide a large decrease in depressive symptoms and can be as effective as a short-term antidepressant medicine treatment for mild to moderate forms of clinical depression.

Antidepressant Medicines

Common antidepressants for postpartum depression:

  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs): Sertraline (Zoloft®) and Fluoxetine (Prozac®)
  • Serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs): Duloxetine (Cymbalta®) and Desvenlafaxine (Pristiq®)
  • Bupropion (Wellbutrin® or Zyban®)
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs): Amitriptyline (Elavil®) or Imipramine (Tofranil®)

Please consult healthcare providers for a proper prescription based on individual needs.
